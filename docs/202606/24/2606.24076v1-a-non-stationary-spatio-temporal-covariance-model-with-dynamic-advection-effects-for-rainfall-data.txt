ABSTRACT 

We propose a non-stationary model constructed using a mixture of spatio-temporal covariance models with advection effects; namely, models that have larger covariance values along an orientation vector in the spatio-temporal index set, that simulate wind direction and cloud movement. We show that a mixture of such models can allow for wind direction change in data during (estimated) time intervals, unlike traditional models that use rigid advection effects. We construct a MCMC procedure for Bayesian estimation, and illustrate the method with the analysis of a severe rainfall event from the southeastern region of Brazil. 

Keywords Spatial statistics, Spatio-temporal data, Bayesian methods, Short-term rainfall forecasting 

1 Introduction 

Separable spatio-temporal covariance models are a restrictive class of covariance functions, defined as the product of a strictly spatial covariance function and a strictly temporal one. While separability allows for easier computation, especially when using likelihood-based approaches Cressie and Wikle (2011, p.310), it is considered an unrealistic assumption, especially in fields such as atmospheric sciences and epidemiology. Non-separable flexible models and methods to assess non-separability of data have been studied in recent years (O’Hagan, 1998; Gneiting et al., 2006; Bourotte et al., 2016; De Iaco et al., 2019). For rainfall data, short term rainfall phenomena may satisfy Taylor’s hypothesis (Taylor, 1938), a less restrictive type of symmetry, for which the correlation decay rate between two distinct locations, as we consider locations further apart, is proportional to a correlation decay in time between measurements at the same location. Taylor’s hypothesis does not always hold, particularly in South American forests (Poveda and Zuluaga, 2005), but it is a reasonable assumption when studying rainfall during short periods for which wind changes have a strong effect in cloud movement (Northrop, 2024). Covariance models with advection effects have been introduced by Gupta and Waymire (1987) and Cox and Isham (1988). Recently, Salvaña and Genton (2020) extended such models to the multivariate case with a Lagrangian framework, including scenarios with multiple advection effects in Salvaña et al. (2023). 

2 Spatio-temporal models with advection 

Let t ∈ T ⊂ R and s ∈ S ⊂ R2. Define space-time covariance function as 

C(s, s′; t, t ′) = Cov (Y (s, t ), Y (s′, t ′)) .

A simple model of space-time covariances is the separable model , where 

C(s, s′, t, t ′) = CS (s, s′)CT (t, t ′).

This formulation has computational advantages: A separable model allows us to choose known spatial and temporal covariance functions. Moreover, separability also allows for an easier computational implementation, in particular with

However, separability implies complete symmetry (CS), that for all s, s′ ∈ Rd and t, t ′ ∈ R, we have Cov (Y (s, t ), Y (s′, t ′)) = Cov (Y (s, t ′), Y (s′, t )) .

Complete symmetry is limiting: assume that clouds travel 25km/h towards east. If we model rainfall using a stochastic process, we expect that correlation between Paraty at noon and Rio de Janeiro around 5pm (approximately 200 km apart) to be higher than dependence between Rio de Janeiro around 5pm and Paraty at noon. However a CS model postulates they are the same. Non-separable stationary and non-stationary spatio-temporal covariance functions can be constructed based on Cressie and Huang (1999), Gneiting (2002), and others. In this work, we focus on the class of functions that satisfy Taylor’s hypothesis (Taylor, 1938): that there exists a vector V ∈ Rd such that 

C(0; u) = C(Vu; 0) .

The units of V are the ratio between units of h and u, space / time, thus V can be interpreted as a speed vector. The advection class of spatio-temporal covariance functions was introduced in Gupta and Waymire (1987), and Cox and Isham (1988) extended it; both cases consider univariate Gaussian random fields (the multivariate case was studied in Salvaña and Genton, 2020; Salvaña et al., 2023). Consider a random field with finite variance 

Y (s, t ) = ˜Y (s − Vt), (s, t ) ∈ Rd × R, V ∈ Rd, d ≥ 1,

where ˜Y (s) is a purely spatial random field, and V is the advection vector that controls speed and transport direction for ˜Y (s). Its spatio-temporal covariance function is given by 

C(h, u ) = CS (h − Vu), h = s′ − s, u = t′ − t, 

where CS is a purely spatial covariance function. We can treat V as a constant vector, and thus the associated random processes are called frozen fields (Gupta and Waymire, 1987). If we treat V as a random variable, the associated random processes are called non-frozen fields (Cox and Isham, 1988). Treating V as a random variable allow the transported random field to show some diffusion in time, unlike the frozen fields which are rigidly transported along V. For example, an exponential covariance model using Gupta and Waymire (1987) formulation would be

In this simulation study, the spatial locations are on random sites (uniformly distributed), but fixed across simulations. • The likelihood function is built using only data observed in S × T ; we will use the data in S × T 1+ to assess prediction. • We repeated each scenario B = 10 independent times. MCMC was run for 1000 steps, with a burn-in period of 200 (this takes roughly 5 minutes per configuration). The results of the simulated study are shown in Figure 1. We obtain stationarity of λ posterior samples without issues, centered on the correct (that is, simulated) values. The parameters θ are sensitive to choice of priors. In this simulation, we used an Exponential(6), so the expected priors are informative, not too far from the “true” values from which data was simulated from ( θ1 = 0 .1, θ 2 = 0 .2). The behavior of the spatial dependence parameter in spatial covariance models is known to require informative priors; see the discussion in Zhang (2004). The Metropolis steps for V tend to almost always reject proposals (we are using random-walk normal proposals). We have thus made very small proposal steps for them, but we will need much larger MCMC chains to get reliable posterior samples of V. Lastly, the estimate of τ is concentrated on the “true” simulated value of τ = 6 , but for L = 2 the posterior distribution of τ is conjugated, and therefore we can conclude that the posterior mass is indeed concentrated on the change point value. To compare predictions, we also implemented MCMC for the Gupta-Waymire model (Gupta and Waymire, 1987) using the same priors and no change function ϕ. We remark that the Gupta-Waymore model suffers from the same issues when posterior sampling θ and V. Prediction is done by averaging ordinary Kriging over the posterior sample of the parameters. The average squared prediction error, and its standard deviation, for the models are shown in Table 1.
