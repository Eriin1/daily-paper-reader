Abstract —Soil moisture measurement plays a key role in irri-gation and environmental management. Yet it remains unreliable due to heterogeneous soils, limited sensing volumes, temperature drift, and parasitic inter-channel coupling. This work presents a compact multi-depth capacitive probe that extends a parallel-plate geometry from previous work with differential activa-tion to suppress stray capacitances and improve accuracy. An equivalent-circuit model quantifies parasitic effects, and optically coupled transistor bridges isolate each sensing layer. Raw capaci-tance is converted to volumetric water content and plant-available water using established calibration models. Laboratory results show a fourfold reduction in temperature sensitivity, strong confinement of the sensing volume, and improved repeatability in heterogeneous soils. Field validation against reference sensors demonstrates high accuracy and precision comparable to widely used instruments, enabling a practical and scalable solution for agricultural and urban soil-moisture monitoring. 

Index Terms —capacitive sensing, soil moisture, FDR sensor, differential sensing, crosstalk reduction, multi-channel sensing 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Climate change alters global precipitation and demands more efficient water use in agriculture and urban greenery [5]. Accurate soil-moisture monitoring enables optimized ir-rigation but remains challenging due to the need for site-specific calibration and poor reproducibility [6]–[9]. Reported limitations include soil disturbance during installation [10], [11], dependence on texture and bulk density [12], [13], small or uneven sensing volumes [3], [14]–[16], and temperature-related drift [17]–[20]. As a result, commercial probes often fail to provide consistent readings in heterogeneous soils, and satellite missions remain limited to surface layers [21], [22]. In practice, in-situ sensor networks combined with IoT technologies are used to capture vertical profiles [23]. These rely on electrical, dielectric, thermal, or tensiometric principles [24]–[29], where raw signals (voltage, frequency, counts) are converted to volumetric water content (VWC) or soil-water potential [29], [30]. VWC is the water–volume ratio [29], [31], while soil-water potential quantifies suction forces of the soil [32]. For irrigation control, values are often normalized as plant-available water (PAW), bounded by field capacity and wilting point [32]–[34]. Because these thresholds vary strongly with texture, from about 5 % VWC for sand to > 30 % for clay [34], soil-specific calibration remains necessary [29]. The absence of a universal conversion standard is still a major obstacle to comparable soil-moisture data. Soil-moisture infor-mation supports precision irrigation and ecosystem resilience in both agriculture and cities [35]–[37]. Because over 75 % of the roots of annual crops and young city trees occur within 

60 cm of the surface [38], [39], sensors covering this range

Natural soils are stratified in texture and density [32], [41], requiring sensing volumes that balance spatial noise and layer separation [42]–[45].The probe geometry in this work is chosen to balance these effects and provide stable, layer-specific measurements. Conventional probes exhibit non-uniform sensing fields biased toward the sensor surface. Ten-siometers measure only at the ceramic tip, see Fig. 1 (a) [1]. Rod-type capacitive and TDR sensors concentrate fringing fields near electrodes, see Fig. 1 (b),(c) [46]–[49]. Water films or compaction during insertion may distort readings [11], [15], [31], [50], [51]. To achieve homogeneous sampling, our previous design [4] used two parallel-plate electrodes inserted from the surface, generating a uniform field through a defined soil slab and allowing multi-depth measurements, see Fig. 1 (d) [4]. This paper presents an improved soil-moisture sensor addressing these issues. A differential sensing scheme sequen-tially isolates electrodes using optically coupled MOSFET bridges, subtracting stray and coupling capacitances verified by a circuit model and node-voltage analysis. In addition, a conversion from corrected capacitance to normalized PAW using soil-specific field-capacity and wilting-point parameters together with laboratory and field validation is presented. II. A. Equivalent circuit model 

Embedding several capacitive sensing zones for multi-depth measurements introduces stray coupling and wiring parasitics between adjacent electrodes, causing inter-channel crosstalk [52], which is quantified using the equivalent-circuit model in Fig. 2. Each channel measures the electrode–soil capacitance 

C0(εr), formed by the insulation capacitances Ciso on both electrodes in series with the soil capacitance Cr(εr), plus a fixed wiring offset Coff . Parasitic couplings to neighbouring channels and the ground shield are modelled as Cp,a (εr, T )

and Cp,b (εr, T ), which depend on relative soil permittivity 

εr and temperature T . To enable subsequent crosstalk com-pensation, ideal switches, modelled as capacitors Csa & Csb ,are included and are first assumed closed (infinite capacitance value). The excitation signal uQ is generated by a capacitance-to-digital converter, which drives the sensor with a sinusoidal signal of 1.2 V pp at 1 MHz to 3 MHz using an internal LC resonant network and measures resonant frequency shifts to infer capacitance changes. The digitized values are read by an on-board microcontroller for capacitance conversion.

A. Design and Construction Summary 

The differential scheme in Eq. 10 suppresses parasitic coupling and temperature drift by alternating electrode switch states. Low off-state capacitance and switch placement directly at the electrode feed are essential to limit stray effects [4]. Op-tically coupled MOSFET bridges were selected for their gal-vanic isolation. The probe uses a four-layer FR4 PCB (shielded traces, gold-plated electrodes) in a polycarbonate housing, re-ducing compaction and improving robustness. Low-duty-cycle operation yields milliwatt-range average power consumption, enabling long-term battery-powered deployments. 

B. Volume Sensitivity 

The dynamic range of the differential measurement Cdiff (εr)

is roughly five times smaller than that of the direct mea-surement Cm(εr, T ) (Table I), based on air and water mea-surements at room temperature for three depths. For sensing-volume characterization, a 1.2 L water-filled latex balloon was placed concentrically in individual probe depths, while the surrounding volume was kept dry. Air, balloon-filled, and immersed states were sampled for 5 min and averaged. The relative volume contribution was computed as the ratio be-tween the balloon-induced and full-scale signal changes. The differential setup captured 93 − 96 % of the dynamic range, compared to 63 − 68 % for direct measurements, confirming improved spatial confinement of the measurement.

D. Repeatability in Real Soil 

The repeatability of the improved soil moisture sensor was evaluated in a sandy field during winter in central Europe. Insertion was standardized with a steel replica of the probe with a ground plate used to pre-form slits before placing the sensor. It was inserted 15 times within 4 m 2, and the resulting capacitance values were compared with gravimetric references from oven-dried samples. Fig. 4 shows the nor-malized capacitances as violin plots. Cm(εr, T ) showed 5 % 

interquartile range (IQR) and up to 15 % deviation, while 

Cdiff (εr, T ) showed 8 % IQR and only 3 % deviation from ground truth, indicating improved accuracy and robustness.

This work introduces a differential sensing approach for soil moisture measurement combined with a PAW computation method. Two optically coupled semiconductor switches alter-nately isolate the electrodes, thereby suppressing superposed parasitic capacitances previously reported in [4], as confirmed by the equivalent-circuit model and closed-form analysis. Dynamic range. Although differential operation reduces the electrical dynamic range by approximately a factor of five (Table I), the remaining span remains above the converter noise floor, corresponding to < 1 % VWC resolution and 

≈ 5 % deviation in field tests (Fig. 4), compared to up to 

15 % for direct measurements. Quantization and long-term drift effects remain below seasonal variability (Fig. 3), so improved volume confinement and thermal stability outweigh the reduced span. The improved volume confinement and thermal stability therefore outweigh the reduced dynamic range in practical deployments. 

Volume sensitivity. The water-balloon experiment confirms that conventional direct measurements include contributions from outside the intended sensing region, whereas the dif-ferential scheme confines the effective sampling volume to the plate-to-plate space. This addresses the issue that small or poorly defined sensing volumes cause spatial variability during installation [6], [14], [51], [69]. The findings align with confined-volume and compartmental/submersion tests [15], [50], [70] and indicate effective crosstalk suppression. 

Temperature stability. The absolute measurement exhibits a thermal response consistent with FR4 stack-up and dielectric behaviour [71]. Differential operation markedly lowers the apparent temperature gradient. Residual drift can be attributed to the properties of the insulation material (polycarbonate) [72] and incomplete thermal equilibrium. In field deployments, temperature-dependent water permittivity will slightly add to this effect [20], [27], [73]. TDR systems typically report permille degree sensitivities [18], [19]. The differential scheme enhances thermal robustness in the system response. 

Repeatability & field evaluation. In sandy soil, the differen-tial mode reduces the systematic offset to gravimetric ground truth with only a modest precision trade-off, consistent with its reduced dynamic range (Fig. 4). Under urban conditions, cross-sensor comparisons show competitive agreement. Fur-thermore, no significant performance degradation due to aging or moisture ingress was observed during the field deployments. These results align with laboratory improvements in volume confinement and thermal stability, and they compare favorably to the variability reported for existing systems. [6], [8]. 

Conclusion. The optical–semiconductor-based differential probe substantially mitigates multi-channel crosstalk and im-proves thermal stability. It also better confines the sampling volume, enhancing accuracy across depths. Combined lab and field evidence suggest a practical path toward more reliable irrigation decisions in agricultural and urban settings. Future work will target precision gains (materials/geometry, signal processing) and systematic evaluation under varying soil prop-erties without sacrificing the demonstrated robustness.
